## The Seasons Are Suggestions

Here's the thing about the best-dates windows on each run: they're our best guess. We're not meteorologists. We don't have a hotline to the snowpack. We're just people who have run these trails enough times to have strong opinions about when they're good.

In reality, a warm El Niño year might have Dale Ball open and dry in February. A stubborn La Niña could leave Winsor Trail buried under two feet of snow in June. The mountains don't read the calendar, and they definitely don't read this website.

**What this means for you:** The season windows are a starting point, not a promise. Before you drive an hour up Hyde Park Road expecting dry singletrack, do a quick check:

- **CalTopo or AllTrails recent reviews** — other runners will say if there's snow.
- **Santa Fe National Forest conditions** — they occasionally post trail updates.
- **Just look up at the mountains.** If the Sangre de Cristos are white, there's a decent chance the high trails are still buried.

If you show up and the trail is a slushy mess or a sheet of ice, that's not a bug — that's New Mexico. Come back in two weeks.

## Difficulty Is Also Just Our Opinion

We rate runs on a ski-slope scale (green / blue / black / double-black / extreme) because it's intuitive and because we're running trails in a ski town. But just like on the mountain, what counts as "easy" depends enormously on who you are.

A fit local who grew up at 7,000 feet will breeze up something we called "Hard" while a first-time visitor still adjusting to altitude might find our "Easy" rating to be a legitimate sufferfest. Both experiences are valid. The mountains are democratic like that.

**The fine print, spelled out:**

- Difficulty ratings reflect our subjective assessment of terrain, distance, and elevation gain for a reasonably fit trail runner acclimatized to Santa Fe's altitude (~7,000 ft).
- All run information on this site — distances, elevation gain, altitude, season windows, difficulty ratings — is provided for general informational purposes only. It may be inaccurate, outdated, or just wrong.
- **You run at your own risk.** Full stop. We are not responsible for anything that happens to you on trail: injuries, altitude sickness, getting lost, bad decisions, acts of God, or encounters with wildlife.
- Always make your own assessment before heading out. If a trail looks beyond your ability on the day, trust that instinct. The mountain will be there next time.

## Actually, Let's Talk About Safety

Okay, friend-talk over for a second. This part is real.

### Altitude

Santa Fe sits at 7,000 feet. Many of these trails climb to 10,000, 12,000, or even 12,600 feet. If you just flew in from sea level, your body hasn't had time to figure out what's happening. Altitude sickness is real: headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness. The cure is to go down, drink water, and give yourself a day or two before hammering the high routes.

Seriously. The mountains are not going anywhere. Take a day.

### Afternoon Thunderstorms

From roughly June through August, the mountains above treeline get hammered by afternoon thunderstorms. They build fast — a clear blue morning can turn into a full-on lightning show by 1pm. If you're doing anything above treeline (Winsor Trail, we're looking at you), start early and plan to be back below the trees by noon at the latest.

If you get caught above treeline in a storm: get off the ridge, get away from lone trees and water, crouch low with your feet together if lightning is imminent. Don't be the tallest thing around.

### Falls

Rocks are slippery. Roots are sneaky. This is probably how most trail running injuries actually happen — not bears, not weather — just a moment of inattention and a face-to-dirt introduction. Especially on the rocky technical stuff in the Dale Ball system and on loose descents in the high country.

Trekking poles are not a sign of weakness. They're a sign that you'd like to keep your ankles.

### Wildlife

Yes, there are rattlesnakes. They like warm rocks and don't enjoy surprises any more than you do. Watch where you put your hands and feet, especially in lower-elevation trails (Dale Ball, Borrego) in warmer months.

There are also black bears, coyotes, and mountain lions in the Santa Fe National Forest. Your odds of a bad encounter are low, but not zero. Make noise on trail (or just breathe loudly, we won't judge), carry bear spray in the high country if it makes you feel better, and don't run alone at dawn and dusk if you can help it.

### Getting Lost

Cell service is unreliable once you're past the trailhead. Download maps for offline use before you go — CalTopo, Gaia GPS, AllTrails, whatever you prefer. The CalTopo links on each run page are a good starting point.

Tell someone where you're going and when you expect to be back. It's old advice. It still works.

---

## The Short Version

Go early. Bring water. Check conditions before you go. Know your limits and be honest about them on the day. Come back down if something feels wrong. The trails aren't going anywhere.

Now go have fun.
